## Part B: Password Security Review

### 1. What makes a strong password?
A strong password is long (at least 12 characters), complex (mix of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and special characters), and unpredictable (does not contain personal info or dictionary words).

### 2. Why should passwords be unique?
If one account gets breached, hackers try those same credentials on every other major website (credential stuffing). Unique passwords isolate the damage to just one account.

### 3. What is Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)?
MFA is a security mechanism that requires a user to provide two or more verification factors to gain access to an account (e.g., a password PLUS a code sent to a phone).

### 4. What are the risks of password reuse?
Password reuse creates a single point of failure. A breach at a low-security online forum could expose credentials that grant access to your critical financial or professional accounts.

### Personal Password Policy (5 Best Practices)
1. Minimum length of 14 characters for all primary accounts.
2. Mix uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols (`@`, `#`, `$`, etc.).
3. Never include personal names, birthdates, or common dictionary words.
4. Use a unique password for every single account without exception.
5. Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) on every platform that supports it.

## Part D: Cyber Threat Research

### 1. Phishing
* **Definition:** A deceptive technique where attackers mimic legitimate organizations via email or message to trick users into revealing sensitive data.
* **Real-world Example:** The 2016 Democratic National Committee (DNC) email leak started via a malicious phishing link sent to John Podesta.
* **Prevention Methods:** Check sender addresses carefully, never click unsolicited links, and use email filtering tools.

### 2. Malware
* **Definition:** Malicious software designed to disrupt, damage, or gain unauthorized access to a computer system.
* **Real-world Example:** The Stuxnet worm discovered in 2010, which targeted and damaged Iran's nuclear program infrastructure.
* **Prevention Methods:** Keep systems updated, use strong antivirus software, and avoid downloading files from untrusted sources.

### 3. Ransomware
* **Definition:** A type of malware that encrypts a victim's files, with the attacker demanding a payment to restore access.
* **Real-world Example:** The 2017 WannaCry attack that crippled NHS hospitals and businesses globally.
* **Prevention Methods:** Maintain regular offline data backups and keep operating systems patched.

### 4. Social Engineering
* **Definition:** The psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or divulging confidential information.
* **Real-world Example:** The 2020 Twitter hack, where attackers called employees pretending to be IT support to gain network credentials.
* **Prevention Methods:** Verify identities through official secondary channels and build a strong culture of skepticism regarding unusual requests.

### 5. Data Breach
* **Definition:** An incident where secure, confidential, or protected data is copied, transmitted, or viewed by an unauthorized individual.
* **Real-world Example:** The Yahoo data breach affecting billions of accounts, exposing names and email addresses.
* **Prevention Methods:** Encrypt sensitive data at rest and in transit, and enforce strict user access control controls.

## Part F: Security Reflection
During my personal security audit, I realized that while my primary email account is highly secure, some of my older social media platforms still use outdated passwords without MFA enabled. This leaves a weak link in my digital footprint. To address this, I will immediately audit all secondary accounts, deploy a password manager to handle unique strings, and turn on app-based MFA wherever possible. In today's hyper-connected landscape, cybersecurity is vital because our identities, financial assets, and professional reputations are entirely digital. A single slip in cyber hygiene can lead to devastating identity theft or financial loss.
